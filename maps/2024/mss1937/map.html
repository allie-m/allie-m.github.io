This map is a follow-up to my 1931 Market Street Subway map that posits instead that the 1935 Market Street Subway proposal (put on the ballot in 1937) was followed through with. It features Market, Mission, and Geary subways!!

It is also designed to be compatible with my Market Street Subway Heavy Rail map.

The Market Street Subway is 2 track, starting at Church to collect the J/K/L/M/N, emerging onto the surface to reach the Embarcadero and also turning south to serve the Transbay Terminal; all lines are routed to the terminal.

The Mission Subway feeds into the Market Street Subway between Van Ness and Guerro, running underground to around 30th St and then emerging onto the surface out to Balboa Park.

The Geary Subway runs from Fillmore to Montgomery, turning up Montgomery and then running on new surface tracks to meet Columbus Ave; the B/C and E/F are interlined much like how the K/T worked in our universe. It is completely separate from the Market/Mission subways, although there is a transfer at Montgomery.

All of these subways are designed for streetcar rolling stock, not fully grade separated rapid transit. The stations can all accommodate up to 3 car trains.

I have editorialized the following century. In brief:

  • The Market Street Railway streetcar lines are mostly converted into (trolley)buses in the 40s/50s, but these lines survive because of their grade separations.
  • The Key System is municipally bought out in 1948 and, in 1956, the organization managing it is merged with the SFMTA to create the San Francisco & East Bay Municipal Railway (SF&EBMR).
  • Treasure Island is redeveloped as a regional airport, to supplement the city's other major airport.
  • By 1962, SF&EB realizes that operating a streetcar-subway with 6 interlined lines is... hard. They pass a bond to deepen the tunnel, adding two more tracks in a double-decker configuration; the bottom tracks are extended underground to meet the Twin Peaks Tunnel. The K/L/M/S are routed through the lower tracks, while the J/N/Mission lines continue to run on the upper tracks.
  • In 1978, the Mission line is extended on its surface right-of-way (same as BART's) out to Daly City, and the K/M are extended a few blocks to meet it.
  • In 1982, SF&EB introduces automatic train control and modernizes the rolling stock to modern, low-floor trams, instead of PCCs. Like in our universe, it has growing pains, but because there are no more than 3 lines for every 2 tracks, it works just fine! Modern light rail :D
  • In the 1990s, California embarks on a massive heavy rail expansion and electrification project (see this heavy rail map, mentioned above).
  • In 2002, the F Berkeley is extended down Solano Ave to the meet the new heavy rail station.
